Moment of inertia is a measure of an object's resistance to changes in its rotational motion. It depends on both the mass distribution and the axis of rotation, playing a crucial role in rotational dynamics.

Common moment of inertia units:
• **kg⋅m² (kilogram square meter)**: The SI unit of moment of inertia. Standard for scientific and engineering calculations.
• **slug⋅ft² (slug square foot)**: Imperial unit used in mechanical engineering, especially in the US.
• **lb⋅ft² (pound square foot)**: Common in engineering applications, though technically incorrect (should use slug).
• **g⋅cm² (gram square centimeter)**: Used for small objects and laboratory measurements.
• **lb⋅in² (pound square inch)**: Used for smaller mechanical components.
• **oz⋅in² (ounce square inch)**: Used for very small components like model parts.

Key relationships:
- I = ∫ r² dm (definition for continuous mass distribution)
- 1 kg⋅m² = 0.7376 slug⋅ft² = 23.73 lb⋅ft²
- 1 slug⋅ft² = 1.356 kg⋅m² = 32.17 lb⋅ft²
- Moment of inertia depends on axis of rotation

Common shapes:
- **Solid disk**: I = ½mr² (about center)
- **Solid sphere**: I = ⅖mr² (about center)
- **Rod**: I = ⅓ml² (about end), I = 1/12ml² (about center)
- **Ring**: I = mr² (about center)

Moment of inertia is important in:
- **Mechanical Engineering**: Gear design, flywheel sizing, shaft calculations
- **Automotive**: Engine balance, wheel design, drivetrain dynamics
- **Aerospace**: Aircraft control, satellite attitude, propeller design
- **Robotics**: Joint motor sizing, arm dynamics, precision control
- **Civil Engineering**: Building dynamics, earthquake analysis

Understanding moment of inertia helps in:
- Calculating torque requirements for rotation
- Designing energy storage systems (flywheels)
- Analyzing vibration and resonance in rotating systems
- Optimizing motor selection for robotic applications
