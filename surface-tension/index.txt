Surface tension is the force per unit length acting along the boundary of a liquid surface. It arises from the cohesive forces between liquid molecules and is responsible for many phenomena like droplet formation and capillary action.

Common surface tension units:
‚Ä¢ **N/m (newton per meter)**: SI unit for surface tension. Standard for scientific and engineering calculations.
‚Ä¢ **dyn/cm (dyne per centimeter)**: CGS unit commonly used in materials science and chemistry.
‚Ä¢ **mN/m (millinewton per meter)**: Practical unit for most liquids, numerically equal to dyn/cm.
‚Ä¢ **J/m¬≤ (joule per square meter)**: Energy per unit area, equivalent to N/m for surface tension.
‚Ä¢ **lbf/ft (pound force per foot)**: Imperial unit used in some engineering applications.

Typical surface tension values (at 20¬∞C):
- **Water**: 72.8 mN/m (high due to hydrogen bonding)
- **Mercury**: 486 mN/m (highest among common liquids)
- **Ethanol**: 22.3 mN/m (lower due to organic nature)
- **Benzene**: 28.9 mN/m (typical organic solvent)
- **Soap Solution**: 25-40 mN/m (reduced by surfactants)

Temperature and contamination effects:
- **Temperature**: Surface tension generally decreases with increasing temperature
- **Surfactants**: Dramatically reduce surface tension (detergents, soaps)
- **Impurities**: Even trace amounts can significantly affect surface tension
- **Critical Temperature**: Surface tension approaches zero at critical point

Surface tension is important in:
- **Chemical Engineering**: Distillation, extraction, bubble columns
- **Materials Science**: Coating processes, adhesion, wetting phenomena
- **Biotechnology**: Cell membranes, protein folding, drug delivery
- **Environmental Engineering**: Oil spill cleanup, flotation processes
- **Manufacturing**: Printing, painting, soldering, cleaning processes

Understanding surface tension helps in:
- Designing efficient separation and purification processes
- Optimizing coating and painting applications
- Developing effective cleaning and decontamination methods
- Understanding biological membrane behavior and drug interactions
