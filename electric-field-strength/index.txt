Electric field strength is the force per unit charge exerted on a test charge placed in an electric field. It represents the intensity of the electric field and is fundamental to understanding electrostatic phenomena and electrical breakdown.

The SI unit for electric field strength is volt per meter (V/m), which is equivalent to newton per coulomb (N/C).

**Key characteristics:**
• **Vector quantity** - has both magnitude and direction
• **Force relationship** - F = qE (force = charge × field strength)
• **Voltage gradient** - E = -∇V (negative gradient of electric potential)
• **Superposition principle** - fields from multiple sources add vectorially

**Fundamental relationships:**
• **Electric force**: F = qE (force on charge q in field E)
• **Potential difference**: V = ∫E·dl (line integral of electric field)
• **Gauss's law**: ∇·E = ρ/ε₀ (divergence relates to charge density)
• **Energy density**: u = ½ε₀E² (electrostatic energy per unit volume)

**Applications:**
• **High voltage engineering** - insulation design and breakdown analysis
• **Electrostatic precipitators** - particle collection efficiency
• **Capacitor design** - dielectric strength and energy storage
• **Lightning protection** - field enhancement and discharge initiation
• **Electron guns** - particle acceleration and beam focusing
• **Electrostatic painting** - coating processes and efficiency

**Breakdown field strengths:**
• **Air (dry)**: ~3 MV/m (atmospheric pressure)
• **Vacuum**: No intrinsic limit (field emission from surfaces)
• **Glass**: 10-25 MV/m (depends on type and purity)
• **Mica**: 100-200 MV/m (excellent insulator)
• **Polyethylene**: 20-40 MV/m (plastic insulation)
• **Transformer oil**: 10-15 MV/m (liquid dielectric)
• **SF₆ gas**: 8-10 MV/m (at atmospheric pressure)

**Common field strengths:**
• **Atmospheric fair weather**: 100-150 V/m (downward)
• **Under thunderclouds**: 1,000-10,000 V/m
• **Van de Graaff generator**: 100-500 kV/m (near sphere)
• **High voltage transmission**: 1-10 kV/m (at conductor surface)
• **CRT electron gun**: 1-10 MV/m (acceleration region)
• **Laser-induced breakdown**: >100 MV/m (optical damage)

**Measurement techniques:**
• **Field mills** - rotating vane electrostatic sensors
• **Kelvin probes** - non-contact surface potential measurement
• **Electric field meters** - calibrated probe instruments
• **Optical methods** - Kerr effect and Pockels effect sensors
• **Spark gap measurements** - breakdown voltage determination

**Design considerations:**
• **Conductor geometry** - sharp edges enhance fields
• **Dielectric properties** - permittivity affects field distribution
• **Temperature effects** - thermal expansion and breakdown strength
• **Humidity influence** - moisture affects breakdown in air
• **Frequency response** - AC vs. DC field behavior

**Safety standards:**
• **Human exposure limits** - typically <5 kV/m for public areas
• **Equipment spacing** - minimum clearances for given voltages
• **Corona inception** - field levels that initiate partial discharge
• **Flashover prevention** - surface contamination considerations

**Related phenomena:**
• **Corona discharge** - ionization at high field regions
• **Partial discharge** - localized electrical breakdown
• **Field emission** - electron emission from surfaces
• **Electrostriction** - mechanical deformation in dielectrics
• **Dielectrophoresis** - particle motion in non-uniform fields

**Key relationships:**
- **Parallel plates**: E = V/d (uniform field between plates)
- **Point charge**: E = kq/r² (Coulomb's law for point source)
- **Cylindrical geometry**: E = λ/(2πε₀r) (infinite line charge)
- **Spherical geometry**: E = Q/(4πε₀r²) (spherical charge distribution)

**Units conversion examples:**
• **1 V/m** = 1 N/C = 0.001 kV/m = 0.01 V/cm
• **1 kV/cm** = 100,000 V/m = 0.1 MV/m = 100 N/C
• **1 MV/m** = 1,000,000 V/m = 1,000 kV/m = 10,000 V/cm

Understanding electric field strength is essential for electrical engineers, high voltage specialists, electrostatics researchers, and anyone working with electrical equipment, lightning protection, or dielectric materials.
