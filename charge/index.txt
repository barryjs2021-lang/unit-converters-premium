Electric charge is a fundamental physical property of matter that causes it to experience a force when placed in an electromagnetic field. It is the physical property responsible for electric and magnetic phenomena.

The SI unit for electric charge is the coulomb (C), named after French physicist Charles-Augustin de Coulomb. One coulomb is defined as the charge carried by a current of one ampere in one second.

**Key characteristics:**
• **Fundamental property** - conserved quantity in isolated systems
• **Two types**: positive and negative charges
• **Quantized**: exists in discrete multiples of elementary charge
• **Source of electric fields** - creates forces on other charges

**Common charge quantities:**
• **Elementary charge (e)**: 1.602×10⁻¹⁹ C (proton/electron)
• **Static electricity**: 10⁻⁹ to 10⁻⁶ C
• **Battery capacity**: 1-200 Ah (3,600-720,000 C)
• **Lightning**: 1-300 C
• **Capacitors**: 10⁻¹² to 10⁻³ C

**Battery capacity examples:**
• **Coin cell (CR2032)**: 220 mAh
• **AA alkaline**: 2,500-3,000 mAh
• **Smartphone**: 2,000-4,000 mAh
• **Laptop**: 40-100 Wh (≈10-25 Ah at 3.7V)
• **Electric vehicle**: 50-100 kWh (≈1,350-2,700 Ah at 37V)

**Applications:**
- Battery and energy storage system design
- Electrostatic discharge (ESD) protection
- Capacitor and electronic component selection
- Electroplating and electrochemical processes
- Particle physics and atomic structure
- Lightning protection and safety

**Important relationships:**
- **Current**: I = Q/t (charge per time)
- **Energy**: E = Q×V (charge times voltage)
- **Capacitance**: C = Q/V (charge per voltage)

Understanding electric charge is essential for electronics, battery technology, electrostatics, and all electrical phenomena in engineering and physics.
