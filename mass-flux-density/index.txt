Mass flux density (also called mass flux) is the mass flow rate per unit area. It represents how much mass passes through a unit area per unit time, commonly used in heat and mass transfer, fluid dynamics, and chemical engineering.

The SI unit for mass flux density is kilogram per square meter per second (kg/(m²·s)). It's fundamental in describing transport phenomena across interfaces.

**Key characteristics:**
• **Mass per area per time** - describes intensity of mass transfer
• **Vector quantity** - has both magnitude and direction
• **Interface property** - describes transport across boundaries
• **Conservation principle** - total mass flux across closed surfaces equals accumulation rate

**Common applications:**
• **Evaporation and condensation** - water from surfaces
• **Mass transfer** - across membranes and interfaces
• **Chemical reactions** - mass transport to surfaces
• **Corrosion studies** - material loss rates
• **Sublimation** - solid to gas phase transitions
• **Diffusion processes** - concentration-driven transport

**Typical ranges:**
• **Evaporation from water**: 0.0001-0.01 kg/(m²·s)
• **Industrial mass transfer**: 0.001-0.1 kg/(m²·s)
• **Membrane processes**: 0.00001-0.001 kg/(m²·s)
• **Chemical vapor deposition**: 0.0001-0.01 kg/(m²·s)

**Related processes:**
- **Heat flux** - thermal energy per area per time
- **Momentum flux** - momentum transfer (viscous stress)
- **Molar flux** - moles per area per time
- **Volumetric flux** - volume per area per time

**Key relationships:**
- **Mass balance**: ∂ρ/∂t + ∇·j = 0 (j = mass flux density)
- **Fick's law**: j = -D∇ρ (diffusion-driven mass flux)
- **Convective flux**: j = ρv (mass flux due to bulk motion)

Understanding mass flux density is essential for process design, environmental engineering, materials science, and any application involving mass transport across interfaces.
