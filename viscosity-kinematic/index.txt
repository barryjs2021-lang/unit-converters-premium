Kinematic viscosity is the ratio of dynamic viscosity to fluid density. It represents the fluid's resistance to flow under gravity and is crucial for fluid mechanics calculations.

Common kinematic viscosity units:
• **m²/s (square meter per second)**: SI unit for kinematic viscosity. Used in scientific calculations.
• **St (stokes)**: CGS unit, equal to 1 cm²/s. Named after Sir George Gabriel Stokes.
• **cSt (centistokes)**: Most common practical unit, equal to 1 mm²/s. Standard for oil and fluid specifications.
• **mm²/s (square millimeter per second)**: Equivalent to centistokes, widely used in industry.
• **ft²/s (square foot per second)**: Imperial unit used in some engineering applications.

Key relationships:
- Kinematic viscosity (ν) = Dynamic viscosity (μ) ÷ Density (ρ)
- 1 m²/s = 10,000 St = 1,000,000 cSt = 1,000,000 mm²/s
- 1 St = 100 cSt = 100 mm²/s = 0.0001 m²/s
- Reynolds number: Re = ρVL/μ = VL/ν

Typical kinematic viscosity values:
- **Water (20°C)**: ~1 cSt (1 mm²/s)
- **Air (20°C)**: ~15 cSt (15 mm²/s)
- **Gasoline**: ~0.6 cSt (0.6 mm²/s)
- **Engine Oil SAE 10W**: ~4-6 cSt at 100°C
- **Heavy Fuel Oil**: 50-500 cSt at 50°C
- **Honey**: ~10,000 cSt (very viscous)

Temperature effects:
- **Liquids**: Viscosity decreases with increasing temperature
- **Gases**: Viscosity increases with increasing temperature
- **Viscosity Index**: Measures rate of viscosity change with temperature

Kinematic viscosity is important in:
- **Lubricant Engineering**: Oil selection, bearing design, gear lubrication
- **Fluid Mechanics**: Pipe flow, pumping systems, heat transfer
- **Automotive**: Engine oil specifications, fuel system design
- **Chemical Processing**: Mixing, separation, heat exchanger design
- **Aerospace**: Hydraulic systems, fuel specifications

Understanding kinematic viscosity helps in:
- Selecting appropriate fluids for specific applications
- Calculating pressure drops and pumping requirements
- Designing lubrication systems and bearing clearances
- Optimizing heat transfer and mixing processes
- Determining flow regimes (laminar vs. turbulent)
