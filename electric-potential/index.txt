Electric potential (voltage) is the amount of electric potential energy per unit charge at a specific point in an electric field. It represents the work done per unit charge in moving a charge from a reference point to that specific point.

The SI unit for electric potential is the volt (V), named after Italian physicist Alessandro Volta. One volt is defined as the potential difference that will cause one ampere of current through one ohm of resistance.

**Key characteristics:**
â€¢ **Relative measurement** - always measured between two points
â€¢ **Drives current flow** - current flows from high to low potential
â€¢ **Energy per charge** - joules per coulomb (J/C)
â€¢ **Fundamental to all electrical systems**

**Common voltage levels:**
â€¢ **Nerve signals**: 70 mV (resting potential)

â€¢ **Household electricity**: 120-240 V
â€¢ **Automotive systems**: 12 V - 48 V
â€¢ **Industrial power**: 480 V - 69 kV
â€¢ **Transmission lines**: 115 kV - 800 kV

**Electronic system examples:**
â€¢ **Logic circuits (TTL)**: 5 V
â€¢ **Logic circuits (CMOS)**: 3.3 V - 1.8 V
â€¢ **USB power**: 5 V
â€¢ **Laptop chargers**: 19 V - 20 V
â€¢ **Server power supplies**: 12 V

**Applications:**
- Electronic circuit design and power supply design
- Power distribution and electrical safety
- Battery and energy storage systems
- Measurement and instrumentation
- Electrical code compliance
- Lightning protection systems

**Safety considerations:**
- **Low voltage**: < 50 V (generally safe)
- **High voltage**: > 1000 V (potentially lethal)
- **Extra high voltage**: > 345 kV (transmission lines)

Understanding electric potential is fundamental to electrical engineering, electronics design, and electrical safety in all voltage-dependent applications.
