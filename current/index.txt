Electric current is the flow of electric charge through a conductor. It represents the rate at which electric charge flows past a point in a circuit, measured as charge per unit time.

The SI unit for electric current is the ampere (A), named after French physicist André-Marie Ampère. One ampere is defined as the flow of one coulomb of charge per second through a conductor.

**Key characteristics:**
• **Flow of charge** - coulombs per second (C/s)
• **Fundamental to all electrical systems**
• **Direction matters** - conventional current flows from positive to negative
• **Can be DC (direct) or AC (alternating)**

**Common current ranges:**
• **Electronic devices**: μA to mA range
• **Household appliances**: 1-20 A
• **Electric vehicle charging**: 16-80 A
• **Industrial motors**: 100-1000 A
• **Power transmission**: kA range
• **Lightning**: 10-30 kA peak

**Applications:**
- Circuit design and electrical safety
- Power consumption calculations
- Battery management systems
- Motor control and drives
- Electronic component ratings
- Electrical code compliance

**Key relationships:**
- **Ohm's Law**: I = V/R (current = voltage/resistance)
- **Power**: P = V×I (power = voltage × current)
- **Charge flow**: Q = I×t (charge = current × time)

Understanding electric current is essential for electrical engineering, electronics design, and electrical safety in all current-carrying applications.
